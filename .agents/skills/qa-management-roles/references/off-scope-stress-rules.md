# Off-Scope Submission / Interview Stress Rules

Cross-cutting rule for both M1 and M2 skill families — referenced from
people-risk, 1to1-prep, and OKR/development-plan skills, rather than
duplicated in each.

## What This Tracks

A person being submitted, interviewed, or asked to rapidly prepare for a
role outside their real competence — a different stack, domain, or
seniority expectation than their actual CV/experience — even when they are
**never placed in that role**. The interview/prep episode itself is the
stressor, not the outcome. A rejected or withdrawn submission does not
close this out; if anything it can be worse, since the person absorbed the
stress with no resulting opportunity to show for it.

This is distinct from normal stretch assignments or agreed upskilling — the
marker is the person's own reaction: language like "this isn't my area,"
visible cramming/prep stress, imposter-syndrome framing, or any sign the
mismatch itself (not the workload) is what's unsettling them.

## Detection

- Do not rely on the person to keep raising it. The pattern this rule
  exists to catch is exactly the one where the person goes quiet about it
  after the acute episode passes, not because it's resolved, but because
  they've stopped expecting anyone to act on it.
- Watch for it surfacing indirectly: a 1:1 mention of a training/mock
  interview, a stack they don't know, a submission "that came out of
  nowhere," or a reference to a specific interview/prep episode months
  after the fact — a person re-describing an old prep episode unprompted
  is itself a signal it never actually settled, not idle small talk.
- If M1 or M2 personally ran a mock/training interview or prep session for
  an off-scope submission, that session is itself a data point for this
  rule, not just administrative overhead to note in passing.

## Elevated-Attention Window: minimum 6 months, from the event, not from silence

Once an off-scope submission/interview-prep episode happens, treat it as a
standing red flag for **at least six months** from that episode, not from
whenever the person last mentioned being unhappy about it. A calm patch
after the episode is not evidence it's resolved — it is very often the
person having gone quiet while still replaying the episode privately. Do
not downgrade or close a risk note tied to this purely on the basis of "no
complaints for N weeks."

## Required Response

- **Log the episode explicitly** in the people-risk traffic-light
  (`m1-people-risk-report`) even if the person seems fine immediately
  after — this is a leading indicator this rule exists to surface early,
  the same logic as the newcomer buddy-assignment rule
  (`newcomer-support-rules.md`).
- **Ask about it directly and periodically** in 1to1 prep
  (`m1-1to1-prep`/`m2-1to1-prep`) throughout the six-month window — a
  direct, specific question ("how are you feeling about the
  <role/interview> from back in <month>"), not a generic
  satisfaction check the person can wave off with "everything's fine."
- **Do not let an apparently stabilized risk note go unconditioned.** If
  the row is downgraded because things look calmer, the note must still
  name the six-month window explicitly and state what would re-trigger
  it — the same way a conditional de-escalation should always name its
  own condition (see `m1-people-risk-report`).
- **Cross-check before any new off-scope submission.** Before submitting or
  interviewing someone for a role outside their real competence again,
  check whether they already have an open or recent (within six months)
  episode on record — don't let a second one stack on top of an
  unprocessed first one.

## Rationale

Added 2026-07 after two separate cases where an engineer's stress from
being submitted/interviewed for an out-of-scope role (different stack or
domain than their real experience) appeared to settle down within weeks,
with no further complaints — and in both cases the person was still
carrying it privately, replaying the episode, and it became a real
contributor to their eventual decision to leave the company months later.
In neither case had the episode been treated as a standing item to keep
checking on; both times the apparent calm was read as resolution instead
of as the person simply not expecting anyone to act on it.
